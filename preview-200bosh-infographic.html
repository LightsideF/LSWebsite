The Situation

“We had driven to London not knowing what to expect — braced for more bad news. My wife and I were in our fifties. The thought of losing our home and having to start again felt devastating.”

What We Did

We structured a full and final IVA. Although the property was valued at £1.4 million, once a previously unregistered loan from neighbours was properly accounted for, the available equity was negligible. We prepared an asset and liability statement demonstrating that a one-off lump sum — funded by those same neighbours, who chose to provide additional funds — would produce a better return than bankruptcy, in which creditors would have received nothing. The settlement was approximately 8p in the pound. The IVA was accepted, the home was protected, and Mr & Mrs B were debt free within ten months of their first meeting with us.

A £1.4m home looks like equity for creditors to pursue in bankruptcy. In Mr & Mrs B’s case it wasn’t. Once the mortgage and a previously unregistered neighbour loan were properly accounted for as prior-ranking secured claims, the equity available to unsecured creditors was negligible — which made a lump-sum IVA, funded by those same neighbours, a better outcome for creditors than bankruptcy would have produced.
Adviser: Priti Shah. Referred by an insolvency practitioner.

Design notes:

  • Three boxes in a row, desktop layout, with minus / equals operators between them. The layout reads as an equation: £1.4m − (Mortgage + Neighbours’ secured loan) = ≈£0.
  • The first and middle boxes (setup) are off-white with a thin grey border — neutral.
  • The middle box is descriptive rather than numeric. There’s no specific pound figure for the mortgage or the neighbours’ loan in the source case, and inventing one would be wrong. Naming the two prior-ranking secured claims is the substantive content — a reader gets the full mechanism at a glance: “the house is encumbered by the mortgage AND by a secured loan from the neighbours, so there’s nothing left for unsecured creditors.”
  • The plus sign in the middle box echoes the minus / equals operators between the boxes, reinforcing the equation framing and signalling that the middle box represents two combined things.
  • The third box (payoff) is gold-accented: pale gold background, thicker gold border, gold value colour. This ties visually to the case stamp in the header (also gold), creating a narrative loop — the stamp says “8p/£1 settled at”, the infographic explains why that was possible.
  • Values use Merriweather serif bold — the numbers (£1.4m, ≈£0) at 1.15rem, the descriptive words in the middle box at 1.05rem so the lines fit comfortably without wrapping awkwardly.
  • Descriptors underneath use Lato at 0.68rem muted.
  • Caption below the row explains the mechanism in prose, bordered above with a dashed separator. Italic muted text, same style as the attribution line at the bottom. The caption was rewritten to match the corrected framing.
  • On mobile (<560px) the three boxes stack vertically with operators rotated inline. Still legible, still tells the same story.
  • No coral anywhere — card internals stay channel-agnostic per v1.02 Section 3.13.
  • Correction from first draft: the original middle box said £335k labelled as “Neighbour loan (properly accounted for)”. That conflated two different numbers: £335k is Mr & Mrs B’s total personal debt (the figure that went into the IVA), not the secured claim against the property. Subtracting unsecured personal debt from a property value isn’t how equity works, so the equation was nonsense even though the prose insight was right. Rebuilt with the descriptive middle box per Priti’s suggestion.
